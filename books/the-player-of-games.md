# The Player of Games

## Verdict

Ambivalent. The writing and Culture setting were strong, but the game-centered premise was hard to believe, the opening was tedious, and the plot was predictable.

## What worked

- Writing multiple orders of magnitude better than Bobiverse without becoming showy
- The Culture universe
- Sentient ships and drones
- A spacefaring civilization adapted to many environments
- The story became much more interesting once Gurgeh left his home planet

## What did not

- Games treated as a serious field of study were hard to accept
- Little personal interest in games
- Written descriptions made the game mechanics hard to visualize
- Detailed game passages made the first chapters a slog
- A civilization built around a game strained suspension of disbelief
- The central outcome felt obvious and predictable

## Reader comments

> OK, just read it through. I'm ambivalent. The writing is multiple orders of magnitude better than the Bobiverse poutine, but it doesn't go into "Look how well I can write" crap.
>
> The Culture universe is interesting. Sentient ships and drones, space faring civilization that actually evolved to manage different environments, that's pretty cool.
>
> I had trouble with my suspension of disbelief on the core story arc, though - mainly games as a serious field of study. I get that there's not much else to actually do within this society, so I understand the idea behind it - I just have trouble with believing it outright.
>
> I found the first few chapters a slog, as they're all about the different games Culture plays, which I couldn't really care about. Just not a big game player myself, but even if I was, I found it hard to represent game mechanics in a writing form. I just couldn't "see" the games, and since they were described in so much detail, it was a bit tedious.
>
> When Gurgeh gets off planet, it gets a lot more interesting, but still - a civilization built on a game is hard to swallow. I also found the story itself a bit predictable as well (he wins all the games and plays the emperor against all odds, then the society collapses - obvious).
>
> I'm not against reading another culture novel, if the story arc is not based on games. Or go completely elsewhere.

## Recommendation implications

### Recommend more of

- The Culture universe when the central plot is not based on games
- Strong, controlled prose that does not call attention to itself
- Sentient ships, drones, and genuinely space-adapted societies

### Avoid

- Detailed game descriptions
- Plots whose central premise must be accepted despite weak personal plausibility
- Predictable “outsider defeats the system” arcs
